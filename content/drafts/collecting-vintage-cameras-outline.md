# A Developer's Guide to Collecting Vintage Cameras

## Target Audience
Developers and tech enthusiasts who appreciate precision engineering. People who've picked up an old camera at a flea market and wondered if it was worth something. Assumes no collecting experience but respects the reader's intelligence.

## Scope
A practical, opinionated introduction to collecting vintage cameras. What to look for, what to avoid, how to start without blowing your savings. Grounded in the same engineering appreciation that draws developers to mechanical keyboards and hi-fi audio.

**In scope:** Why cameras are collectible, main categories, evaluating condition, pricing, where to buy, 8-10 iconic cameras, red flags, basic maintenance.
**Out of scope:** Detailed repair guides, digital camera collecting, investment portfolio strategy, lens collecting (separate rabbit hole).

## Key Sections

### Introduction (2-3 sentences)
If you've ever admired the engineering of a Swiss watch or a mechanical keyboard, vintage cameras are the next rabbit hole. This guide helps you fall in wisely.

### Why Developers Love Cameras
- Precision mechanical engineering — gears, springs, no batteries required
- Optical design — physics you can hold in your hand
- The satisfaction of understanding how something works by taking it apart (and hopefully reassembling it)
- The analog-digital parallel: these are the "bare metal" of photography

### The Main Collecting Categories
Brief overview of what's out there:

#### 35mm Rangefinders
- The jewels of the camera world: Leica M, Contax G, Canon P, Voigtländer Bessa
- Compact, quiet, beautiful
- Price range: $100 (Canon Canonet) to $10,000+ (Leica MP)

#### 35mm SLRs
- The workhorses: Nikon F, Canon AE-1, Pentax K1000, Olympus OM-1
- Abundant, affordable, great for actually shooting
- Price range: $30-500 for most

#### Medium Format
- The "wow" cameras: Hasselblad 500C/M, Rolleiflex 2.8, Mamiya 7, Pentax 67
- Bigger negatives, bigger prints, bigger prices
- Price range: $200 (Yashica Mat) to $5,000+ (Hasselblad)

#### Large Format
- The "are you serious?" cameras: Speed Graphic, Linhof Technika, Sinar
- More of a practice than a collection — these are big
- Niche but fascinating

#### The Oddities
- Subminiature cameras (Minox), spy cameras, half-frame (Olympus Pen), Polaroid SX-70
- Often the most fun to collect — weird, cheap, conversation starters

### How to Evaluate Condition
The practical stuff:
- **Shutter:** Does it fire at all speeds? Listen for consistency
- **Light seals:** Almost always need replacement on old SLRs — not a dealbreaker, $15 fix
- **Lens:** Fungus, haze, separation, scratches — what matters and what doesn't
- **Viewfinder:** Brightness, clarity, frame line accuracy (rangefinders)
- **Body:** Brassing (wear on brass bodies) — cosmetic, often adds value to collectors
- **Film door:** Light-tight? Hinges intact?
- **Meter:** If it has one, does it work? Often the first thing to die, least important to fix (use a phone app)
- The grading scale: Mint, Excellent+, Excellent, Good, Fair, Parts — what sellers actually mean

### Where to Buy (and Where to Be Careful)
- **Reputable dealers:** KEH, Japan Camera Hunter, Bellamy Hunt, UsedPhotoPro
- **Japanese sellers on eBay:** Often the best condition and most honest grading
- **Local camera shops:** Becoming rare, but great when you find one
- **Estate sales and thrift stores:** The treasure hunt — low prices, no guarantees
- **Avoid:** Facebook Marketplace for expensive items, "untested" listings on eBay (usually means broken), sellers with no return policy
- **Japan auctions** (Buyee, Jauce): access to incredible stock, but factor in shipping and customs

### 10 Cameras Worth Knowing
Curated list — a mix of icons, sleepers, and entry points:

1. **Nikon F** — The professional's SLR. Built to survive a war (and did).
2. **Canon AE-1** — The camera that democratized SLR photography. Affordable, abundant.
3. **Olympus OM-1** — Small, beautiful, mechanical. The developer's camera.
4. **Leica M3** — The one that started it all. Still a joy to use.
5. **Hasselblad 500C/M** — Medium format perfection. Modular, tank-like, iconic.
6. **Rolleiflex 2.8F** — Twin-lens reflex magic. Waist-level shooting changes your perspective.
7. **Contax T2** — The titanium compact. Zeiss lens, gorgeous rendering. (Warning: prices have gone insane.)
8. **Pentax K1000** — The teaching camera. Fully mechanical, fully reliable, fully cheap.
9. **Polaroid SX-70** — Instant photography as industrial design. Folds flat.
10. **Minolta CLE** — The "poor man's Leica." Compact rangefinder, Leica M mount, fraction of the price.

### Red Flags and Scams
- "CLA'd" without documentation (CLA = Clean, Lubricate, Adjust — costs $150-300, should have a receipt)
- Fungus described as "slight haze"
- "Rare" versions that aren't actually rare
- Replaced parts on "original" cameras
- Film-tested photos that look suspiciously too good (might be different camera)
- The Contax T2/T3 price bubble — be aware of what you're paying for

### Storage and Maintenance Basics
- Silica gel, not a drawer — humidity is the enemy
- Fire shutters periodically — mechanisms seize when unused
- Don't store with batteries in (corrosion)
- Leather cases can trap moisture — better to display openly
- When to get a CLA and who to trust (mention a few well-known technicians)

### Getting Started Without Going Broke
- Start with a $50-100 camera that shoots (Canon AE-1, Pentax K1000, Olympus OM-1)
- Shoot a few rolls — collecting is better when you understand the tool
- Set a budget before browsing eBay at midnight
- One camera at a time, learn its quirks, then expand
- The "daily shooter" philosophy vs. the "display case" philosophy

### Summary
Collecting cameras is collecting engineering history. Each one is a snapshot (literally) of what a group of engineers thought was the best way to capture light at a specific moment in time. Start cheap, learn the mechanics, and let the collection find you.
